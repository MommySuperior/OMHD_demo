🧠 What’s New in Digital Mental Health Research 
To stay current with digital mental health research, I’ve identified a handful of recent studies that offer practical and timely insights for anyone working at the intersection of mental health, technology, and care delivery, particularly for youth and individuals with complex clinical needs. Here are a few that stood out:

📍 1. Early engagement matters
🔗 https://lnkd.in/g37_AKSh
Among young people using an unguided anxiety self-help app, those who completed more tasks early on were more likely to keep using the app, regardless of their initial anxiety severity.
Why it matters:
Focusing on onboarding and early interaction, through personalization, reminders, or gamification, can improve long-term engagement and outcomes.

📍 2. Combining digital tools with face-to-face therapy improves results
🔗 https://lnkd.in/gqVy_4xb
An RCT found that adding a mobile app to traditional CBT was more effective than CBT alone for key clinical outcomes in college students.
Why it matters:
Blended care approaches can extend the reach and effectiveness of therapy, especially for young adults.

📍 3. AI should be transparent and support, not replace, human connection
🔗 https://lnkd.in/gUfZ997n 
Young people find AI-driven resource recommendations acceptable only when it’s clear the interaction isn’t human, preserving the value of real peer and clinician support.
Why it matters:
Use AI thoughtfully, as a tool for signposting and support, with clear communication about data use and without mimicking empathy.

📍 4. Social media recruitment can be faster, cheaper, and more inclusive
🔗 https://lnkd.in/gcZ6e6sU
Recruiting participants via social media was quicker, more cost-effective, and reached a more diverse group, including higher-risk individuals, compared to traditional methods.
Why it matters:
Leveraging targeted video ads, detailed landing pages, and pixel tracking can help researchers and programs reach underserved and vulnerable populations more effectively.
