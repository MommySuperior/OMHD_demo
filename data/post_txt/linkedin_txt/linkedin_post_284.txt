Considering hiring a mental health professional to provide services to your athletes?   Since the term “sport psychology” is applied liberally to a vast field of professionals it is often difficult for those within the sports industry to understand whether they are working with someone who is licensed to practice psychology and can diagnose and treat mental health problems, or someone trained to apply mental-preparation techniques to enhance athletic performance.  Here are some important points:

- For mental health (which also improves mental performance) hire full-time, part-time or as a consultant a licensed mental health professional for referrals and consultation. These professionals are often licensed to practice psychology, typically with a PhD in clinical or counseling psychology, or a masters-level clinician with a degree in counseling or social work; trained to identify, diagnose, treat mental-health problems; and have additional experience in athletics or with athletes.

- If you hire a "sports phycologist" - someone trained to apply mental-preparation techniques to enhance athletic performance only (e.g. PhD in Kinesiology), look for additional professional competency such as Certiﬁed Mental Performance Consultants® (CMPCs).  Just remember these individuals should be trained in how to refer to your mental health provider.

- Check the individual's credentials and licensure within your State to make sure they care for patients/clients/athletes.  This is a State-by-State licensure profession.  This includes telehealth visits in most states as well as "fly in" consultants.

- For example, in the State of Maryland, an individual can not call themselves as sports psychologist without a license to practice psychology.  This is not the same in every State.  Be wary, know who you are hiring.  

- Many professionals in this space are cash only and do not accept insurance so have a plan and put it in writing.  

- Know the rules.  For NCAA sports, actions of an individual that could make them a "countable coach" include actions such as shagging balls, assisting with drills, throwing batting practice, signaling in plays, serving as a practice player, setting up offense and defense alignments, or walking out to the pitching mound for player consultation during a game.

- A “non-coaching” sports psychologist might be allowed at practice or sit on the bench of competitions as long as they are simply observing and not performing coaching activities. 

- Develop and rehearse a Mental Health Emergency Action Plan.

- Free National Suicide Prevention & Crisis Lifeline - Text or Call 988 - available 24 hours a day.
