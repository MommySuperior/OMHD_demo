May is mental health month. Thanks to two of our Cleveland Clinic Canada corporate advisory partners Susy Michor from TD Bank and Karen Smith, MBA from Bruce Power for their open discussion on mental health in the workplace at the HRMI conference in Muskoka. 

A few key points on this important topic:
- ~47% of employees reported experiencing burnout symptoms in 2025
- ~70% of Canadian employees say their productivity was negatively impacted by their mental health in 2025
-  The financial impact on Canadian Employers is measured in billions annually, and mental health disorders have become the leading cause of long-term disability claims.

Many organizations have made substantial investments in mental health, yet our outcomes have not improved. 

Ultimately employees need to feel safe in asking for help, and leaders can help create that safety through their visible support. 

And not only is early mental health support important, but so is prevention. We can improve an employee’s mental health by simply making the workplace a great place to work. We know this is often achieved by preventing burnout and focusing on:
 - creating community & connection in the workplace
- allowing for control & autonomy in one’s work
- working on meaningful work
- through reward & recognition
- fairness & flexibility in the workplace

When benefits match a strong education and culture, that’s when we truly begin to move the needle.
