If you care about student mental health—and especially outcomes in community college work—this research is a must-read.

A new CCMH study by Scofield & Abelson takes a deeper look at how counseling centers differ between two-year/community colleges and four-year institutions—and the findings should prompt serious reflection across our field. 

Here’s what stood out to me:
🔹 We’re serving fewer students—but with equal (or greater) strain: 
Community colleges are reaching a smaller share of their student body with counseling services, even while clinicians carry slightly higher caseloads.
🔹 Access, not demand, may be the limiting factor
We know community college students experience significant mental health challenges—often compounded by financial stress and barriers to care. 
🔹 Different models are filling the gaps: Compared to four-year institutions, community college centers are more likely to:
Use session limits and less frequent appointments
Lean into peer support, coaching, and screening tools
Serve in co-responder roles alongside campus safety
🔹 Infrastructure gaps persist
Less access to professional affiliations, case management, and clinical vendor supports raises important questions about long-term sustainability and student outcomes.

So what do we do with this?
For me, this study reinforces a simple but urgent reality:
👉 We cannot achieve equitable student success without rethinking how mental health systems are designed, resourced, and delivered in community colleges.

A call to action for leaders, practitioners, and partners:
➡️ Pause and assess: Are we truly meeting the mental health needs of our students—or just those who can access services as currently structured?
➡️ Re-examine models: Is our “dose” of care aligned with outcomes we expect (retention, persistence, completion)?
➡️ Invest intentionally: Are we resourcing counseling, case management, and upstream supports at levels that reflect the realities of our student population?
➡️ Engage collectively: How can we partner—across institutions, systems, and organizations—to close these gaps?

Community colleges educate nearly half of all undergraduates—and often those with the greatest barriers.

We have an opportunity—and a responsibility—to build mental health systems that truly meet that moment.

💬 I’d welcome perspectives from others in the field—what are you seeing on your campus, and what’s shifting in your model right now?
