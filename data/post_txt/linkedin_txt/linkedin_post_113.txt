Mental health support in humanitarian settings should prioritise practical, scalable care over complex diagnostic frameworks and intensive psychotherapies. 
Our recent correspondence in The Lancet Psychiatry outlines why focusing on the diagnosis and treatment of personality disorders is not feasible during major emergencies. Instead, we advocate for transdiagnostic approaches that employ brief, skills-based interventions to enhance functioning, strengthen emotional regulation, and build resilience across a wide range of mental health conditions.
The most effective way to meet diverse needs, including personality-related difficulties, in low-resource and emergency contexts worldwide is to expand workforce capacity through task-sharing approaches within a multi-layered system of care. With the current dramatic funding reductions for humanitarian assistance, we must do everything possible to ensure broad and equitable access to essential mental health and psychosocial support for all who need it.
