Yesterday, Coast Mental Health CEO Keir Macdonald appeared before the Select Standing Committee on Finance and Government Services.

The message is clear, it is not about asking government to spend more. It is about delivering better outcomes for people and better value for taxpayers by investing differently.

Across BC, people are cycling between homelessness, hospitals, emergency departments, shelters and correctional facilities. This cycle is costly, ineffective and preventable, and reflects persistent gaps in housing, supports and pathways to stability.

We urge government to take a more strategic approach and focus investments where they can have the greatest impact by advancing three key priorities:

1. Invest in the right housing with the right supports
People need access to housing that matches their needs at the right time. Today, too many are forced into options that do not support their recovery because key parts of the continuum are missing or the right supports are unavailable. We see people completing treatment with nowhere appropriate to go, others remaining in higher levels of care because there are no alternatives, and many cycling back into crisis as a result.

2. Invest in pathways to employment, education and community participation
Housing and health care are essential, but they are not enough. People also need opportunities to build meaningful, connected lives. Today, only 10 to 15 per cent of people living with severe mental illness are employed, despite many wanting and being able to work with the right supports. Better aligning existing investments can help create real pathways to employment, education and community inclusion.

3. Strengthen pathways from custody back to community
Correctional facilities are increasingly managing challenges they were never designed to address. One of the biggest barriers people face upon release is not access to health care, it is access to housing. While Community Transition Teams play a critical role, there is an opportunity to strengthen their impact by embedding housing expertise. This would help ensure people leave custody with a pathway to stability, not a return to homelessness.

The path forward is clear.

By shifting where and how we invest, we can reduce crisis, improve outcomes and build a more effective and compassionate system for people living with mental illness in British Columbia.
