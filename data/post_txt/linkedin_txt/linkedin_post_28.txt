What happens when insurance-based mental health care is built into Ob/Gyn care? 
⠀
In our new study, we examined access to treatment for Women’s Mental Health @Ob/Gyn, an insurance-based mental health service embedded within Ob/Gyn practices. 
⠀
Analyzing nearly three years of data from more than 2,600 women, we asked: Does integration combined with accepting insurance improve access and sustained engagement? 
⠀
Key findings: 
⠀
• 𝐒𝐭𝐫𝐨𝐧𝐠𝐞𝐫 𝐚𝐜𝐜𝐞𝐬𝐬 𝐭𝐡𝐚𝐧 𝐞𝐱𝐩𝐞𝐜𝐭𝐞𝐝: 65% of referred women initiated care—well above rates reported in comparable programs. 
⠀
• 𝐌𝐞𝐚𝐧𝐢𝐧𝐠𝐟𝐮𝐥 𝐬𝐮𝐬𝐭𝐚𝐢𝐧𝐦𝐞𝐧𝐭: Over one-third attended three or more visits, with the highest likelihood of sustained engagement among pregnant, postpartum, and women who had experienced miscarriage or stillbirth. 
⠀
• 𝐁𝐫𝐨𝐚𝐝𝐞𝐫 𝐫𝐞𝐚𝐜𝐡: Nearly 40% of those who initiated care were neither pregnant nor trying to conceive, underscoring unmet mental health needs across the Ob/Gyn lifespan. 
⠀
• 𝐏𝐞𝐫𝐬𝐢𝐬𝐭𝐞𝐧𝐭 𝐬𝐭𝐫𝐮𝐜𝐭𝐮𝐫𝐚𝐥 𝐛𝐚𝐫𝐫𝐢𝐞𝐫𝐬: Gaps in insurance coverage—particularly plans that cover physical but outsource for mental health care—significantly limited sustained engagement. 
⠀
The takeaway is clear: embedding insurance-based mental health into Ob/Gyn care is both feasible and impactful. At the same time, barriers in coverage continue to constrain the full potential of behavioral health integration. 
⠀
I’m grateful to our clinical teams, collaborators, and funders who made this work possible. We hope these findings contribute to policy and systems changes that honor the WHO’s adage: there is no health without mental health.
