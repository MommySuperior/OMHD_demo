Children and Young People’s Mental Health Week is a powerful reminder that supporting emotional wellbeing must begin early. We know that most mental health conditions begin before the age of 25 and prevalence is increasing, which is why a life course approach, starting with strong foundations in early childhood and adolescence, is central to our mission.

We are investing a further £7 million for 24 Early Support Hubs across England offering flexible, drop-in support for 11-25-year-olds. Whether its counselling, psychological therapies, or wider advice on sexual health, education, employment, substances and financial concerns, these services are about ensuring young people can access help early, without pressure or stigma.

Through the 10 Year Health Plan, I’m proud to say we are also continuing to expand Mental Health Support Teams in schools and colleges and expect to reach full national coverage by 2029. These teams provide early, practical support where young people already spend their day, helping them manage worries before they become overwhelming.

But early support is not just about services. Parents, carers, teachers and peers play some of the most important roles in a young person’s mental health, often long before clinical professionals become involved. When children and young people feel heard, understood and supported, at home and in their communities, difficult emotions become easier to manage and seeking help becomes far less daunting. Guidance from Every Mind Matters shows one of the simplest things we can all do to support children and young people is to listen and connect.

This week, as schools, families and communities come together to raise awareness, the message is clear: we all have a role to play supporting children and young people’s mental health and helping every child and young person feel safe and that they belong.
