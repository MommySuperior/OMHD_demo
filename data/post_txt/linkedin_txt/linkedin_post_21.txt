These statistics are not merely numbers—they are signals of a workplace mental health crisis.

In the past 30 days alone:
• 51% of employees reported crying due to work stress.
• 52% experienced anxiety or panic-like symptoms at work.
• 65% felt compelled to hide their struggles to avoid appearing weak.
The last statistic concerns me the most.

When employees believe they must suffer in silence, something is fundamentally wrong with the culture. Whether in academia, healthcare, government, or the corporate sector, people should not have to choose between protecting their well-being and protecting their professional image.

Behind every percentage is a person—a colleague, faculty member, manager, healthcare worker, researcher, parent, or friend—trying to carry invisible burdens while continuing to perform, contribute, and persevere.

Mental health is not a personal weakness. It is a workplace issue, a leadership issue, and increasingly, an organizational responsibility. Perhaps the question we should be asking is not, “How resilient are our employees?”
Instead: “What are we doing that requires so much resilience in the first place?”
